STYLE

Illustrative realism with a historical Art Nouveau foundation, combined with the visual authority of prestige historical manga: expressive hand-drawn inkwork, confident contour lines, dramatic yet naturalistic composition, and highly observed organic texture. The result should feel like a richly illustrated fantasy scene created with traditional ink, wash, and printmaking techniques rather than a polished digital painting.

Render the image as a vintage lithograph, etched book plate, or hand-printed fantasy illustration on aged, lightly textured parchment. Preserve visible fine paper grain, subtle imperfections, and a tactile printed quality.

Use precise dark-brown, charcoal, and sepia ink linework with intentional variation in line weight. Major forms—trees, roots, pond banks, moss, crystalline acorns, stones, and distant trunks—should have clear, elegant, readable silhouettes. Use selective interior linework to describe bark grain, cracks, folds, moss texture, reflected water, crystal facets, and natural weathering.

Prioritize expressive black-and-sepia ink drawing: energetic but controlled hatching, dry-brush textures, stippling, feathered shadows, and occasional bold areas of dark ink. The scene should look hand-illustrated first and realistically observed second. Materials should feel convincing and tactile, but should be simplified into deliberate visual planes, sculptural silhouettes, and carefully designed patterns rather than photographic detail.

Use graceful Art Nouveau geometry throughout: elongated organic forms, flowing whiplash curves, rhythmic branching structures, and botanical motifs embedded naturally into trees, roots, moss, rocks, water, and foliage. These features should arise from the anatomy of the setting, never appear as added ornament.

Create depth through overlapping forms, controlled atmospheric fading, clear foreground-to-background tonal separation, selective cross-hatching, and restrained watercolor-like washes. Keep background forms softer, lighter, and more simplified than foreground forms. Preserve occasional calm areas of open parchment texture and readable negative space.

Keep ornamentation restrained and structural. Do not create a dense floral frame, wreath, halo, border, or crowded corner bouquets. Allow only sparse foreground foliage and a few understated flowers near lower corners or side edges. The visual focus should remain on the grove itself, especially the pond, canopy, and sleeping-dragon tree.

Favor a grounded, slightly asymmetrical composition with deliberate visual shapes, breathing room, and quiet graphic clarity. The atmosphere should feel magical, antique, tactile, and emotionally charged, like a masterfully inked fantasy illustration rather than a decorative poster.

Use a faded but more varied palette: weathered sepia, antique cream, soft charcoal, faded teal, blue-green moss, dusty gold, muted terracotta, pale sage, faded indigo, smoke-violet, soft rust, and subdued olive. Use small controlled highlights of pale silver, cool blue-white, and warm parchment gold for fireflies, water reflections, and crystalline acorns. Colors should remain desaturated, aged, and harmonized rather than bright or neon.

Avoid smooth digital painting, glossy rendering, cinematic realism, airbrushed gradients, flat vector art, heavily decorative borders, excessive flowers, high saturation, photorealism, and overly clean or sterile linework.

No text, lettering, title, labels, runes, symbols, typography, decorative captions, or poster-style border.
